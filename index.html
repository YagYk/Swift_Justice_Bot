Constitution Of India

The Constitution of India is the supreme law of the country, laying the foundation for its political principles, procedures, and powers of government institutions. It was adopted on November 26, 1949, and came into effect on January 26, 1950, a date celebrated as Republic Day in India. The Constitution establishes India as a sovereign, socialist, secular, and democratic republic, ensuring justice, liberty, equality, and fraternity for all citizens. It consists of a Preamble, 22 Parts, 395 Articles, and 12 Schedules, making it one of the lengthiest written constitutions in the world.

Court Fee Structure

The court fees structure in India is designed to cover administrative costs and varies based on the type of case and the court where it is filed. Fees are generally lower in lower courts and increase for higher courts, with specific exemptions and reductions available for certain types of cases or individuals, such as those unable to afford the fees.

Few Imp Cases

India has witnessed several landmark cases that have shaped its legal landscape: Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973): This case established the "Basic Structure Doctrine," ensuring that Parliament cannot alter the fundamental framework of the Constitution. Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978): This case expanded the interpretation of the right to life and personal liberty under Article 21, emphasizing that these rights cannot be restricted except by just, fair, and reasonable laws.
Shah Bano Case (1985): A significant case regarding Muslim personal law, where the Supreme Court granted alimony to Shah Bano, leading to a national debate on the uniform civil code.

Food Safety

The Food Safety and Standards Act (FSSAI) of 2006 is the main law in India that regulates food products, including their manufacture, storage, distribution, sale, and import. The FSSAI Act establishes the Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI) to: Set science-based standards for food Enforce food safety standards Appoint food safety authorities at the state level.

Property Related

The Transfer of Property Act, 1882 is a law in India that governs the transfer of property, including real estate and land. It establishes a framework for property transfers, including the rights and obligations of the parties involved, and legal mechanisms for enforcing transfers. The Act applies to both movable and immovable property, and covers a wide range of transactions, including sales, gifts, mortgages, and leases.

Law against Murder

In India, the law on murder is covered in Section 302 of the Indian Penal Code (IPC): Definition A person is considered to have committed murder if they intentionally cause the death of another person, or if they cause bodily injury that is likely to result in death. The intention to cause death doesn't need to be premeditated. Punishment The punishment for murder is either life imprisonment or death, and the convicted person may also be liable to pay a fine. The death penalty is only given in the rarest of cases.

Common Questions

Here are some of the most common questions that we get.
How to File a Civil Suit in India a. Drafting of Plaint: The foremost step to initiate a suit is to draft a plaint. A plaint is a written document containing allegations. The party who files/ drafts it is known as Plaintiff, and the party against whom the said plaint is filed is known as Defendant. b. Vakalatnama: Vakalatnama acts as a written document, by way of which the parties to the suit authorizes an Advocate to represent them before the respective court. However, if in case a party is personally representing its own case, then there is no need to file a Vakalatnama. c. Filing of Plaint: In the next step, the plaintiff needs to file a plaint before the Chief Ministerial Officer at the filing counter, together with an appropriate court fee and process fee. d. Court Fees: Court fees acts as a nominal percentage of the total value of the suit or the total value of the claim. The amount of court fees and stamp duty varies for every suit, and the same is specified in the Court Fees Stamp Act.
Can I file a case without a lawyer? Yes, an individual can file a case without a lawyer, i.e., party-in-person. However, it is always suggested to hire a lawyer for the case, as an individual can not be expected to be well-versed with the court procedure.
Does a summon goes on record? No, a summon does not go on Court Record.
What happens if a person never gets a summon? If in case a person has not been properly served by the opposite party or by the court, he or she don't need to show up, i.e., the court does not have any personal jurisdiction over that person, and it cannot pass judgment against him or her. However, at the same time, if the court is of the opinion that the said person was properly summoned, it may pass an order in against of the person.
